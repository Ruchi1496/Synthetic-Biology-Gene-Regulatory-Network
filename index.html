Introduction

Gene Regulation Gene regulation is the mechanism by which the production of speci c gene products(protein nor RNA) is increased or decreased in a cell. Developmental pathways can be triggered by modifying the gene expression pat- terns of the cells. All points of gene expression can be regulated. Transcription, RNA processing and transport, translation and post-translational modi cation of a protein, and mRNA degradation are the parts of gene regulation. Regula- tion of Transcription: A cell determines when a gene is transcribed by regulating transcription. A cell increases or decreases amount of mRNA generated from each gene. There are ve di erent ways that RNA polymerase (the enzyme that makes RNA from a DNA template) can be a ected: 1. The speci city of RNA polymerase for promoters can be changed by a cell using speci city fac- tors. These regulatory proteins will make RNA polymerase more or less likely to bind to a promoter. 2. Repressor proteins can bind to an upstream coding sequence, known an operator, to inhibit gene transcribing by RNA polymerase.. This leads to decreased expression of a gene. 3. Activator proteins can bind to the operator region to increase the anity of RNA polymerase towards the promoter. 4. Regulatory regions that lie far upstream to their target gene are called enhancer regions. Activator binding to an enhancer leads the DNA to form a loop, which puts the enhancer next to its target gene. This creates in- creased expression of the target gene. 5. To completely turn o expression of a gene, cell uses silencer regions. The gene is silenced when a silence region is bound by transcription factors. This implies that there is no expression of the gene.

2.1 Operon

In bacteria cistrons or structural genes produce enzymes of a metabolic path- way. These are organised in a cluster which have related fucntions. Poly- cistronic genes of prokaryotes plus their regulatory genes constitute a system called operon. Operon is a unit of expression and regulation.

1.Lactose Operon or Lac Operon
This is a negative control mechanism. The synthesis of enzyme (3-galactosidase has bee5n studied in detail. This enzyme breaks down lactose into glucose and galactose. -galactosidase is present in minimal amounts in the absence of lactose. As soon as lactose is added from outside, the production of - galactosidase is in- creased by thousand times. As soon as the lactose is consumed, the production of the enzyme drops again. The enzymes whose production can be increased by the presence of the substrate are called inducible enzymes. When lactose is added to the culture medium of E. coli, it induces the formation of three enzymes (5-galactosidase, permease and transacetylase, which breaks down lac- tose into galactose and glucose. The genes, which code for these enzymes form a cluster and are called cistrons or structural genes. They are transcribed at the same time into a single mRNA chain, which has codons corresponding to all the three enzymes. The mRNA transcribed from many genes is called polycistronic. Regulatory genes control the functioning of structural genes to produce mRNA. There are three structural genes Z, Y and A, which code for enzymes p-galactosidase, lac permease and transacetylase respectively. Regulatory genes has Regulator I, a control gene called operator gene O and promoter P. I gene produces a protein called repressor or inhibitor. The repressor being active, binds to the operator O and switches o the operator. Thus, the transcription is stopped. When the inducer (lactose) is supplied from outside, the inducers binds to the repressor. The l vbactose changes into allolactase on entering the bacteria. Al- lolactose changes the shape of the repressor which makes it inactive. It cannot bind to the operator then. The operator becomes free and is \turned on" which starts the transcription. The presence of the inducer allows the transcription of Lac operon, as it is not blocked by the repressor protein. The synthesis of enzymes due to the presence of speci c substrate (lactose) is called induction. It is also called de-repression.

2.Tryptophan Operon
It is also a negative control system but it also forms a biosynthetic pathway. It is known as repressible system. It works on the clause that when the amino acid tryptophan is present, the tryptophan operon need not be activated. The co-repressor activates the repressor protein and it binds the operator and switches it \o '. Tryptophan is synthesized in ve steps, where each step re- quires a speci c enzyme. The genes which encodes these enzymes lie next to one another, called trp B, trp D, trp E, trp C, trp A. Tryptophan operon codes for ve enzymes for the synthesis of amino acid tryptophan. In repressible system, the regulatory gene produces a repressor protein, which is normally inactive and doesn't bind to operator on DNA. The repressor on joining the co-repressor undergoes conformational changes that activate it and enable it to bind to the operator. This doesn't allow RNA polymerase enzyme to bind to the promoter. This is opposite to the situation of lac operon in which the repressor is active on its own and when it binds to the inducer, loses the anity for the operator. Operon is regulated by tryptophan and this represses the synthesis of tryp- tophan. The end product of the metabolic chain stops the synthesis of en- zymes.This allows the bacteria to synthesize enzymes only if needed. This is known as feedback repression. In feedback inhibition the end product of a metabolic pathway serves as an allosteric inhibitor of the rst enzyme of the metabolic chain. Induction and repression prevent the synthesis of unnecessary enzymes and save valuable energy.

Gene regulation is about which genes are controlled by which cells and out of the many genes in its genome, which are "turned on". Each cell has a unique set of active genes even when all the cells have exact same DNA. All the steps in eukaryotic gene expression can be regulated. Genes are regulated at multiple steps.

Chromatin accessibility
The chromatin structure can be regulated. More open chromatin makes a gene more available for transcription.

Transcription
Transcription is a key regulatory point for many genes. Sets of transcription fac- tor proteins bind to specific DNA sequences in or near a gene and promote or re- press its transcription into an RNA.

RNA processing
Splicing, capping, exit from nucleus and addition of a poly-A tail to an RNA molecule can be regulated. Alternative splicing make different mRNAs from the same.

Stages of eukaryotic gene expression
1).Chromatin structure. Chromatin may be tightly compacted or loose and open. 2).Transcription. A primary transcript is made by an available gene by tran- scription. 3).Processing and export. The primary transcript is processed (spliced, capped, given a poly-A tail) and sent out of the nucleus. mRNA stability. In the cytosol, the mRNA may be stable for long or may be quickly degraded (broken down). 4).Translation. The mRNA may be translated by ribosomes to make a polypep- tide. 5).Protein processing. The polypeptide may undergo various types of process- ing, including proteolytic cleavage (snipping off of amino acids) and addition of chemical modifications, such as phosphate groups. For a given gene, all these steps need to be executed for an active protein to be present in the cell.

RNA stability

Translation

Protein activity

4. Gene Regulatory Network

Gene regulatory network (GRN) is a cumulation of molecular regulators that interplay among themselves and with other materials in the cell to control the gene expression levels of mRNA and proteins. The regulator can be DNA, RNA, protein and complexes of these. The synergy can be direct or indirect . It can be through transcribed RNA or either through a translated protein. Each mRNA molecule goes on to make a particular protein. Sometimes, this protein will in- corporate at the cell covering or within the cell to give it particular design prop- erties. In other cases the protein will be an enzyme. Some proteins trigger other genes, and these are the transcription factors that play the main role in regula- tory networks.By binding to the promoter region at the start of other genes they make them start initiating the production of another protein. Some transcrip- tion factors are inhibitory. The GRN is modelled as a directed graph; G = (V, E) , where V represents the set of all nodes (genes) and E is the set of all edges (the interaction between a pair of genes). An edge e in set E represents physical and/or regulatory inter- actions between two genes.Genes can be viewed as nodes in the network, with input being proteins such as transcription factors, and outputs being the level of gene expression. The node itself can also be viewed as a function which can be obtained by combining basic functions upon the inputs. Thus by modelling gene regulatory network we get weight matrix giving the output of interactions between two nodes. Now using these output weight matrix value we can cal- culate the value of gene expression required for a particular change in a cell.[4]

6. Random Forest Regressor Algorithm

Random forest Algorithm works as large collection of decorrelated trees. This algorithm makes use of lots of decision trees to make a classi cation. These de- cision trees are developed on the basis of random selection of data and random selection of variables.[6] How do we create random trees? We create lots of random subsets from the sample set of original yeast micro dataset array. We create decision trees from these randomly chosen subsets. For making decision trees, we enter training sets (X,y) where X is the 2D array of all the inputs genes and their feature values and y is the array of features of the corresponding target genes. [2] We plot the corresponding points of the array on X-Axis and Y-Axis. We divide the X-Axis into several partitions and each partition point forms decision node. We classify the dataset according to the decision tree and for each partition or class we calculate the average mean squared error based on the y values. Using mean squared error we nd the weight from the decision tree. The weights obtained from several decision trees are compared and the minimum value is chosen as the nal weight and entered into the featureimportancesmatrix:

These gene networks help in solving a numerous di erent biological and biomed- ical problems. They are discussed below[1]: Causal map of molecular interactions: The most recent use found of gene regulatory networks is to act as a blueprint of the molecular interactions. By this, a gene network can be used to derive an ideal biological hypotheses about molecular interactions, such as for the transcriptional regulation of genes experimented in wet labs. In such a condi- tions GRNs represent causal bio-chemical interactions since the predicted links are to correspond to actual physical binding events between the molecules. A signi cant side of this use is that the GRNs represent statistically signi cant predictions of potential molecular interactions which are obtained from very large-scale data. Given a very large- scale number of potential molecular inter- actions between 20,000 genes in Human beings and 6000 gene in organisms like yeasts, the GRNs are of huge help in pruning these numbers down to potential interactions for which statistical support is available. This, in turn enables more e ective and precise experiments. Networks as biomarkers: Recently, it has been debated that sub-networks can also be utilized as biomark ers, for example for diagnostic and predictive and prognostic purposes. This will be possible for any complex disorder such as cancer, as the hallmarks of cancer genes are represented by pathways instead of individual genes. The aspects of cancer pathway is that their constituting genes are continuously ac- tively interacting with one another. Due to this network-based biomarkers can be represented as statistical measures that will consider the molecular interac- tion structure between individual genes. In contrary to biomarkers which are based on individual genes, they neglect these completely Comparative network analysis When multiple regulatory networks of di erent physiological and other disease conditions will become available, we would be able to statistically com- pare these networks. This will help us learn and understand about molecular interaction changes across di erent physiological and other disease conditions and enhance our biomedical understanding of such phenotypes. However, for this approach to work it would be of necessity to create databases similar to the protein structure databases, this will provide free access to the gene regulatory networks from all di erent physiological and various diseased conditions. Network medicine and drug design: For structuring an ecient network medicine, it will be crucial to mix di erent types of gene networks with one another other as each network type contains in- formation regarding di erent molecular aspects.
